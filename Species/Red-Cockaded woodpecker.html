Red-Cockaded Woodpecker

(Photos from U.S. Fish and Wildlife service, right is a picture of Red-Cockaded Woodpecker chicks)

Description

Standing at 18 to 20 cm tall and with a wingspan of 35 to 38 cm the red-cockaded woodpecker has a black back with white strips. The head is covered in a black cap and males have a red strip the goes along the side of this black cap (Service).

Habitat and Location

The red-cockaded woodpecker can be found throughout the south-eastern U.S. In particular it can be found in the North Carolina counties of found in Anson, Beaufort, Bertie, Bladen, Brunswick, Camden, Carteret, Chatham, Columbus, Craven, Cumberland, Currituck, Dare, Duplin, Edgecombe, Forsyth, Gates, Greene, Halifax, Harnett, Hertford, Hoke, Hyde, Johnston, Jones, Lee, Lenoir, Montgomery, Moore, Nash, New Hanover, Northampton, Onslow, Pamlico, Pender, Pitt, Richmond, Robeson, Sampson, Scotland, Tyrrell, Wake, Washington, wayne, and Wilson counties (Service).

The bird prefers to live inside the trees of the south-eastern pine ecosystem. It'll find an older pine tree, preferably one infected with fungus to make the bark softer. It will then drill a hole into the tree and will nest there with four other non-mating birds during breeding season. Creation of these homes are actually rare so most compete for existing drilled out trees (Finseth).

Reasons For Endangerment

Due to changes in the amount of wild-fires and destruction of the long-leaf pine ecosystem these birds are loosing the trees that they use for homes. In addition their populations have become highly fragmented. Their excavations are used by at least 27 different species in the ecosystem, so they are a critical species to protect. Currently fire management practices are being used to help preserve the forests the red-cockaded woodpeckers call home (Finseth).
